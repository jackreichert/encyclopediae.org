How It Starts

You don't need a hundred universities. You need five, in the same discipline, going deep.

The sequencing problem

Dense Before Broad

Every network platform faces the same early challenge: it is most valuable when everyone is on it, but no one wants to join until everyone is already there. The wrong response is to try to cover everything thinly from the start. The right response is to go deep in one place until the value is undeniable — then let that depth pull in the next wave.

For encyclopediae, that means launching with three to five institutions covering two or three disciplines with real depth — not a thin spread of institutions across all of human knowledge. When Edinburgh, Amsterdam, and LSE all have substantial entries on the same topic in political philosophy, and those entries are visibly, meaningfully different, the platform has demonstrated something no amount of theory can: that institutional diversity produces better knowledge.

What the resistance tells us

Why Top-Tier Resistance Is a Signal, Not a Problem

When we spoke to academics at the world's most prestigious institutions, a pattern emerged. The resistance wasn't intellectual — no one argued that diverse encyclopedias would produce worse knowledge. The resistance was positional. Institutions that have spent centuries accumulating brand authority understand, instinctively, that a platform built on substantive competition is a threat to brand-based authority.

The institutions most eager to participate were those with genuine expertise that the current knowledge infrastructure undersells. A platform that competes on substance is exactly what they've been waiting for.

This is useful information. It means the network's early adopters — the universities who see an opportunity rather than a threat — are precisely the institutions with the most to prove. Motivated participants build better platforms than reluctant ones.

The build sequence

Three Phases to a Self-Sustaining Network

  1. 01
    3–5 institutions  ·  2–3 disciplines  ·  Year 1–2

    Prove the Comparison

    The first phase is not about scale. It is about producing a single undeniable demonstration: that two institutions, writing independently about the same topic, produce entries that are both rigorous and meaningfully different.

    The target disciplines for launch are those where institutional tradition most clearly shapes scholarly perspective — philosophy, economics, political theory, history of science. These are fields where Edinburgh's Enlightenment heritage, LSE's economic tradition, or Leiden's legal history produces a genuinely different entry than any other institution would write.

    • 3–5 founding institutions, selected for complementary strengths and genuine motivation
    • Shared platform infrastructure, each institution maintaining editorial independence
    • 200–500 entries per institution in launch disciplines
    • Public side-by-side comparison as a core interface feature — make the difference visible
    • Academic press coverage as the primary distribution channel
  2. 02
    15–20 institutions  ·  8–12 disciplines  ·  Year 2–4

    Make Absence Costly

    The second phase is where network effects begin to work in both directions. As more institutions join, coverage deepens and the platform becomes a destination for researchers — not just a novelty. At 15–20 institutions, most major academic topics have at least two or three institutional voices.

    More importantly, absence becomes legible. When Edinburgh, Leiden, McGill, and fourteen other universities have encyclopedia entries on a topic, the institution that doesn't is noticeably missing from the conversation. For universities that care about their international standing — and all of them do — this is a meaningful cost.

    • Expansion to STEM disciplines — chemistry, physics, computer science — where institutional research specializations are sharpest
    • Library consortium contribution model (following the SEP/SEPIA precedent) to spread infrastructure costs
    • First endowments established at founding institutions, creating permanent funding independence
    • Annual competitive report: which institutions are most-cited, most-referenced, most-revised
  3. 03
    30–50 institutions  ·  Full coverage  ·  Year 4–7

    The Tipping Point

    At thirty to fifty institutions, the platform reaches escape velocity. Joining is no longer an experiment — it is table stakes for any research university that takes its public knowledge mission seriously. This is the threshold at which arXiv became the default for physics preprints, the threshold at which JSTOR became indispensable to libraries.

    This is also the phase where the early-adopter institutions — the ones who built when building was a risk — have established a several-year lead in content depth, contributor relationships, and editorial reputation. The network's prestige hierarchy has partially reordered: not by brand, but by the quality and depth of what each institution has actually published.

    • Top-tier institutions join — not as leaders, but on the same terms as everyone else
    • Cross-institutional citation and reference data becomes a new academic impact metric
    • The platform's editorial standards become an independent mark of quality
    • International expansion: non-English institutions and multilingual publishing

The institutions that join first set the standard everyone else will be measured against.

That is not a marketing line. It is how academic prestige has always worked — the journals, presses, and repositories founded earliest by committed institutions became the ones everyone else aspired to publish in. The window to be a founder, rather than a follower, is open now.

Why it compounds

The Flywheel

More institutions mean more entries. More entries mean more researchers using the platform. More researchers mean more academics willing to volunteer — because their work will actually be read. More volunteer academics mean better entries. Better entries attract more institutions.

But the compound effect that matters most is reputational. Every time a researcher cites an encyclopedia entry rather than a Wikipedia article, that is a data point. Every time a journalist links to an institutional encyclopedia rather than a general-purpose one, that is a data point. Enough data points and the platform's credibility becomes self-reinforcing — independent of any single institution's prestige.

Institutional Commitment

Founding institutions need to be willing to build, not just join. That means an editorial team, a platform investment, and a genuine willingness to publish entries that reflect the institution's own scholarly perspective — not a consensus view.

Disciplinary Depth

The best founding institutions are those with concentrated excellence in two or three disciplines. A university trying to cover everything at once will produce thin entries. A university going deep on its genuine areas of strength will produce entries worth reading.

Editorial Independence

The platform works only if institutions maintain their own editorial voice. Shared infrastructure, independent judgment. The point is not consensus — it is the productive friction of competing scholarly traditions working on the same questions.

Long Horizon

This is a decade-long project, not a product launch. The institutions best suited for Phase One are those whose leadership understands that the value of an encyclopedia compounds — and that the endowment model exists precisely to match the funding horizon to the knowledge horizon.

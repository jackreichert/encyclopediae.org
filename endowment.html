The Endowment Model

Why permanent capital is the only funding model that makes permanent knowledge possible

The proof of concept

It Already Works

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy has been the definitive global reference for academic philosophy since 1995. It is free to read, rigorously peer-reviewed, written by the world's leading scholars, and updated continuously. It costs $214,000 per year to operate — funded by a $4.125 million endowment. Three paid editors coordinate the work of over 1,500 volunteer academics. This is not a proposal. It is a proof of concept that has run for three decades.

The competitive landscape

Who This Is Really For

When we presented this idea to academic administrators, the response divided cleanly along prestige lines. Universities at the very top of the global rankings were cautious — even resistant. The institutions that immediately grasped the opportunity were the ones ranked just below: world-class universities with deep domain expertise that rarely translates into public recognition.

Harvard doesn't need a platform to establish authority. If Harvard's economics department publishes something, the world already listens. But the London School of Economics — home to more Nobel laureates in economics than most countries — publishes into the same Wikipedia article as everyone else. The University of Edinburgh pioneered the Scottish Enlightenment and leads in AI research today. Leiden is one of Europe's oldest universities. Padua is where Galileo taught. These institutions have centuries of intellectual tradition and current research excellence that the existing knowledge infrastructure fails to surface.

An encyclopedia endowment isn't a cost. It's a competitive investment.

A $100 million endowment funds a world-class, open-access encyclopedia permanently. At Harvard, that represents 0.18% of existing endowment assets — the equivalent of a mid-tier named facility. For a university with a $5 billion endowment, it's 2%. For that institution's reputation and reach in its areas of genuine strength, it could be transformative.

How endowments work

Permanent Capital, Permanent Knowledge

A university endowment is not a budget line. The principal is never spent — it is invested permanently, and only the annual returns flow to operations. At the standard 5% annual payout rate, a gift given today funds operations indefinitely, adjusting with inflation over time. This is why endowments are the correct funding model for knowledge infrastructure: they align the time horizon of the money with the time horizon of the work.

Advertising-funded knowledge serves advertisers. Subscription-funded knowledge serves subscribers. Endowment-funded knowledge serves no one but the mission. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy carries no advertisements, charges no fees, and answers to no commercial interest — because the people who funded it gave capital, not a contract.

The numbers

What It Costs to Build One

At a 5% annual payout, the formula is simple: divide the desired annual operating budget by 0.05 to find the required endowment principal. Below are four realistic tiers for a single-institution encyclopedia.

Credible flagship $3M – $5M $60M – $100M 20–40 staff across editorial, engineering, and outreach. 5,000–20,000 peer-reviewed entries. Full-featured platform with search, versioning, and APIs. Open access.
Comprehensive $5M – $10M $100M – $200M 40–80 staff. Multi-discipline coverage matching the university's full range of expertise. Multilingual publishing, active entry commissioning, integration with library databases.
Network infrastructure $10M – $20M $200M – $400M Cross-institutional coordination layer, shared technology platform, editorial standards body, and support for smaller institutions joining the network. arXiv-scale with editorial depth.

Editorial Staff

40–60% of any encyclopedia budget goes to people. A credible $5M/year operation supports 25–40 staff: an editor-in-chief, managing editors per discipline, editorial assistants, and a small platform engineering team. Volunteer academics write the entries; paid staff coordinate, commission, and review.

Technology Platform

20–35% covers platform build and maintenance — typically $500K–$2M one-time, then $300K–$800K/year ongoing. This includes hosting, search infrastructure, version control, contribution workflows, and APIs. An open-source stack (MediaWiki, custom CMS) substantially reduces cost.

Peer Review Infrastructure

Academic rigor is the differentiator. Software for managing submissions, revisions, and editorial decisions runs $20K–$100K/year. Modest reviewer honoraria — $100–$500 per entry — incentivize participation without replacing the intrinsic motivation that drives academic voluntarism.

Operations & Governance

Legal, compliance, accounting, and administration typically run 12–15% of nonprofit budgets. For an endowed fund within a university, much of this overhead is absorbed by the institution — often at a negotiated rate of 6–12% rather than the commercial standard.

Establishing an endowment

How Universities Actually Do This

The SEP endowment was built in three phases over roughly a decade: a challenge grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities ($500K, conditional on matching funds), a lead gift from private Stanford donors ($1.125M), and a global library consortium campaign — the Scholarly Publishing and Academic Resources Coalition for the SEP (SEPIA) — through which over 600 libraries contributed amounts as small as $100 each to collectively raise $3M.

The same architecture applies at larger scale. An encyclopedia initiative typically begins with an anchor donor or institutional commitment (12–24 months), a library consortium or member-institution campaign (18–36 months), and ongoing faculty, alumni, and named-fund contributions. State matching programs — Kentucky's Bucks for Brains, Texas TRIP, and others — can double private gifts. Foundation co-investment from Mellon, Simons, or MacArthur has seeded every major academic knowledge project of the past thirty years.

A realistic timeline from concept to operational endowment: 3–5 years. That is a meaningful commitment. But an endowment, once established, runs indefinitely. The question is not whether it is worth doing. The question is whether the institution that does it first will be the one that set the standard — or the one that followed.
